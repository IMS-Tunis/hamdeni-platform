5.2a ‚Äî Understand the concept of a digital currency and how digital currencies are used

  • Most digital currencies rely on a Central banking system.
  • It works like regular money but is exchanged electronically between computers over the internet.
  • Digital currency can be transferred between accounts when making transactions, just like cash or credit cards.
  • It is often used through websites and smartphone apps such as PayPal or Apple Pay.
  • Although digital currency exists only on computer systems, it can still be converted into physical cash if needed.
  • Common methods for using digital currency include credit card payments, mobile phone payments, and smart watch payments. The most frequent method used online is credit card payment.
  • A major issue with centralised systems is maintaining confidentiality and security of online payments.
  • Confidentiality is one of the major risks in centralised digital currency systems, along with data security.
  • When you make a payment using digital currency, the data is Encrypted, making the transaction safe.
  • The encryption used in these payments is handled by the HTTPS protocol.
  • Your payment details can also be saved in a Persistent cookie, so you don‚Äôt have to enter them again.
  • A Cryptocurrency was designed to solve the problems of centralised systems by using a decentralised approach.
  • Unlike normal digital currencies, Cryptocurrency transactions are not managed by central banks or governments.
  • Instead, the rules and operations of the cryptocurrency are controlled by a community of users, not a central authority.
  • All cryptocurrency transactions are made public, which allows tracking of payments and monitoring of the total money in circulation.
  • Cryptocurrencies are often considered more secure against tampering because they use a system called blockchain to manage transactions without relying on a central bank.
    • Think of digital currency like invisible money in a video game ‚Äî you can‚Äôt hold it, but it buys things, and it zips across the internet faster than a superhero!
  • When you shop online using credit cards, mobile payment apps like Apple Pay, or PayPal, you are using digital currency that is stored as data in an online account and transferred electronically. This means money moves from one account to another without any physical cash exchanging hands.
  • If your payment details are stored in a persistent cookie, they will be filled in automatically the next time you shop on the same website, so you don't have to type them again.

5.2b ‚Äî Understand the process of blockchain and how it is used to track digital currency transactions

  • A Blockchain ensures that each new transaction is securely connected and traceable.
    • All members of the blockchain network store the same copy of the transaction records, making it very difficult for anyone to secretly change the data.
    • Any changes to a blockchain record must be approved by a consensus of users on the network, which increases security and reduces the risk of hacking.
  • Blockchain technology is used not only for cryptocurrency but also in other fields like smart contracts, pharmaceuticals, education, and politics.
  • A blockchain is made up of many Blocks.
    • Each block contains a Hash value to identify the data and detect any tampering.
    • Each new block also records a Timestamp, helping prove when the transaction occurred.
    • The first block in the chain is called the Genesis block.
  • If someone tries to change the data in a block, it alters the hash and breaks the link to the next block, making the entire chain invalid from that point onward.
    • Changing one block in a blockchain is like switching a Lego piece in the middle of a tall tower ‚Äî the whole thing gets wobbly and might collapse!
  • To prevent fast tampering or fake blocks, a system called Proof-of-work is used. For example, in Bitcoin, it takes around 10 minutes for a computer to solve the required problem and generate a new block.
    • Special users called Miners are responsible for checking and validating new blocks before they are added to the blockchain.
  • When a new block is created, it is sent to all the computers in the network. These computers verify that the data is correct before the block is added.
  • New users joining the blockchain network receive a full copy of all the existing blocks to stay updated and consistent with the rest of the network.
  • A typical Digital currency transaction using blockchain includes the following steps:
    • A digital ledger is used to record the transaction.
    • A device encrypts the transaction data.
    • The encrypted data is sent to the digital ledger.
    • Transactions are stored in blocks, and a single block may contain multiple transactions.
    • When a block is full or the transaction set is complete, it is added to the blockchain and shared across all devices in the network.
  • Because everyone has a copy of the blockchain and all records are encrypted and linked, if someone tries to change a payment record, the change would be obvious to everyone, preventing fraud.
  • In a blockchain: Block 1 (genesis block) has no previous hash. Block 2 has its own hash (e.g., "6AB1") and points back to Block 1. Block 3 has its own hash (e.g., "34EE") and points back to Block 2 ("6AB1").
  • If someone changes Block 2's data, its hash will change (e.g., from "6AB1" to "XXXX"). Block 3 still points to the old hash, making the chain break and invalidating the entire chain from that point forward.
