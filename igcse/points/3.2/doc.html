3.2 Input and Output Devices

Computer Science IGCSE notes, prepared by Dr. Hamdeni

3.2a: Input Devices

  • An input device is a piece of hardware that allows data to be entered into a computer system.
    • The data entered may be in different forms, such as text, images, sound, or movement.
    • Input devices are essential for sending information into the computer so it can be processed.
    • Some input devices are used by humans (e.g., keyboard), while others like sensors are automatic and work in real-time environments.
  • A barcode scanner is used to read barcodes made of dark and light parallel lines that represent digits using specific patterns.
    • Each digit has a unique pattern of thick and thin lines; digits on the left and right of the barcode use different codes to support two-way scanning.
    • When scanned, the barcode reflects red light differently between dark and light areas, which is captured by sensors and turned into digital data.
    • The barcode is looked up in a stock database where its record includes item name, price, quantity, and reorder level.
    • Barcode systems update stock levels and trigger automatic reordering when stock is low.
  • A QR code (Quick Response code) is a square-shaped matrix of black and white blocks that can hold much more data than a traditional barcode.
    • A QR code can store up to 4296 characters or 7089 digits, including web addresses or phone numbers.
    • It includes three large alignment squares in the corners to help the scanner determine the position and angle of the code.
    • QR codes are often scanned using smartphone or tablet cameras, and decoded by QR applications into readable information.
    • QR codes use built-in error-checking so they are more reliable than traditional barcodes even if damaged.
  • A digital camera captures light using a lens and a light-sensitive array of pixels called a CCD (charge-coupled device).
    • Each pixel converts light into an electric signal, which is sent to an analogue-to-digital converter (ADC) to create a digital image.
    • Digital cameras also measure colour using 24-bit RGB: 8 bits each for red, green, and blue levels.
    • More pixels mean higher image resolution and larger file size; quality also depends on the lens and sensor.
    • Images can be transferred to computers using USB or Bluetooth and stored in formats like JPEG or RAW.
  • A keyboard is the most common method of entering text into a computer or device.
    • Pressing a key completes a circuit that the CPU detects and matches to a character using its ASCII value.
    • Keyboards can be wired or wireless; virtual keyboards exist on phones and tablets using touch screens.
    • Repetitive use of keyboards may cause injuries such as RSI (repetitive strain injury), but ergonomic keyboards help reduce strain.
  • A microphone captures sound by detecting air vibrations and converting them into an electric signal.
    • Inside the microphone, a vibrating diaphragm causes a coil to move in a magnetic field, generating a current.
    • This analogue signal is then converted to digital using an analogue-to-digital converter (ADC) before the computer can store or process it.
    • Microphones can be built into the device or connected externally via USB or Bluetooth.
  • An optical mouse detects movement by capturing multiple images per second of the surface below it.
    • A red LED reflects light from the surface; a CMOS sensor reads the pattern and a processor calculates movement.
    • Optical mice are more reliable than mechanical ones and can be used on most flat surfaces.
    • Wireless optical mice use Bluetooth, but wired ones avoid signal loss and don‚Äôt need batteries.
  • Scanners convert physical documents or 3D objects into digital images for storage or processing.
    • 2D scanners use a CCD and light to scan flat pages, producing JPEG or editable text files using OCR (optical character recognition).
    • 3D scanners scan solid objects across x, y, and z dimensions, producing 3D models for CAD design or 3D printing.
    • 2D scanners are often used in airports to match passport photos with live images using face recognition software.
    • The process of 2D scanning involves shining a light on the document, then using mirrors and lenses to reflect the image onto sensors, which convert it into digital format.
  • A touch screen is an input device that detects user interaction through direct touch on the display surface.
    • Capacitive screens use a conductive layer that detects changes in electric fields caused by fingers or special styluses.
    • In a capacitive touchscreen, the screen stores electrical charge and detects coordinates when a finger touches the screen and completes the circuit.
    • Infrared screens detect when a beam of light is interrupted by touch.
    • Resistive screens consist of two layers that create a circuit when pressed together.
    • Touch screens are used in mobile phones, tablets, ATMs, and kiosks to allow direct interaction without a keyboard or mouse.
  • In supermarkets, barcode scanners are used to identify products during checkout. Each scan decreases the stock level in the database. When it reaches the reorder level, new stock is ordered automatically.
  • A QR code can link to a product website. When scanned by a mobile phone, it opens a webpage instantly without typing the URL. This is commonly used in advertisements, boarding passes, or menus.
  • When a photo is taken, the CCD captures brightness and colour of each pixel, like an orange pixel with values red=215, green=165, blue=40. This data is converted to binary and stored as a digital image.
  • When the letter ‚ÄòH‚Äô is pressed, it completes a circuit that sends a signal to the computer, which uses a lookup table to match the signal to the ASCII code for 'H'.
  • Saying ‚Äúhut‚Äù into a microphone produces air vibrations that are turned into electric signals, then digitized into binary so the computer can store or edit the sound.
  • At airport immigration, a 2D scanner reads the passport photo and compares it with a real-time camera photo using software that measures face features like eye distance and jawline.
  • Projective capacitive screens used in modern smartphones allow multi-touch gestures (like zoom or scroll) and work with gloved hands or styluses.

3.2b: Output Devices

  • An output device is hardware that shows or plays the result of the computer‚Äôs processing.
    • Output can be in the form of text, images, sound, or physical actions.
    • These devices allow the system to communicate with the user or another system.
    • Some output devices create visible displays (like screens), while others perform physical tasks (like actuators).
  • An actuator is a mechanical component that performs an action, such as moving a part or turning something on or off.
    • Actuators are often used in systems that need physical changes, like controlling valves or motors.
    • A solenoid is one type of actuator that creates motion using magnetic fields.
    • Solenoids can produce linear or rotary motion depending on their construction.
    • Actuators receive signals from the microprocessor and respond by carrying out actions such as turning on a motor, opening a valve, or switching on lights.
  • Projectors are used to display digital output on large screens and come in two main types: DLP (digital light processing) and LCD (liquid crystal display).
    • DLP projectors use a chip with thousands of tiny mirrors (DMD) that reflect light to form an image.
    • DLP mirrors tilt toward or away from light to produce light or dark pixels and switch rapidly to create greyscale and colour.
    • LCD projectors use coloured mirrors and separate LCD panels for red, green, and blue. These combine to form a full-colour image.
    • DLP projectors are known for high contrast and reliability; LCD projectors give better colour detail and energy efficiency.
  • Inkjet printers spray tiny droplets of liquid ink onto paper using either thermal bubble or piezoelectric methods.
    • A thermal bubble printer heats the ink to form bubbles that force ink onto the page.
    • A piezoelectric printer uses vibrating crystals to eject ink droplets.
    • Inkjet printers are ideal for printing photos or small documents in colour.
    • The print head in an inkjet printer moves back and forth across the paper, precisely controlling where each droplet of ink lands to form the image or text.
  • Laser printers use dry toner powder and static electricity to produce fast and high-quality printed pages.
    • A rotating drum is charged and then scanned by a laser to remove charge in the shape of the image.
    • Toner powder sticks to the charged areas, then is transferred to paper using opposite charges.
    • A heated roller called a fuser melts the toner to make the print permanent.
    • Laser printers are typically used in offices due to their high printing speed, cost-efficiency over time, and ability to handle large print volumes.
  • A 3D printer creates solid objects by adding material layer by layer, based on a digital design.
    • The process starts with a CAD (computer-aided design) model that is converted into a printable format.
    • Direct 3D printing uses inkjet-like movement to build the object vertically.
    • Binder 3D printing sprays powder, then a glue layer to solidify it.
    • 3D printers use materials like resin, metal powder, or plastic, and may require post-processing such as curing or cleaning.
  • Display screens can be LED, LCD, or OLED depending on how they produce images.
    • LED screens use red, green, and blue light-emitting diodes. Brightness is controlled by the current, allowing vivid colours.
    • LCD screens use liquid crystals and need backlighting, usually with LEDs, to display the image.
    • OLED screens use organic compounds that emit light when powered and do not need backlighting.
    • OLED screens are flexible, thin, and use less power, which makes them ideal for smart devices and modern TVs.
  • A loudspeaker is an output device that produces sound by converting electric signals into air vibrations.
    • A DAC (digital-to-analogue converter) turns digital audio files into analogue electrical signals.
    • These signals are amplified to a strong current and sent to a speaker cone.
    • The vibrating cone moves air to create sound waves that humans can hear.
    • Sound output may also be delivered through headphones, which use similar principles as loudspeakers but are designed for personal listening.
  • In an automated irrigation system, an actuator can open or close a water valve when triggered by the system.
  • In a classroom, LCD projectors are often used for presentations because they show sharp and colourful images on a whiteboard.
  • When printing a colour photo, the inkjet printer uses four colours (cyan, magenta, yellow, black) and mixes them in precise amounts to form different shades.
  • Laser printers are used in offices to print hundreds of pages quickly, such as documents or flyers.
  • A prosthetic limb cover can be made using a 3D printer by layering powdered plastic and binding it into a custom-fit shape based on a scan of the user‚Äôs leg.
  • A curved OLED TV uses organic layers to produce bright colours and deep contrast without needing a separate light source.
  • When a song is played on a computer, the audio file is converted to an electric current, sent through an amplifier, and used to move the speaker cone to produce music.

3.2c: Sensors

  • A sensor is a special input device that detects physical changes in the environment and sends that information to a computer system.
    • Sensors detect changes such as temperature, light, pressure, or movement and send continuous signals, not only when changes happen.
    • The data captured by sensors is analogue, meaning it has many possible values and needs to be converted to digital using an ADC (analogue-to-digital converter).
    • In control systems, feedback is used: the output affects the next sensor reading, creating a loop that adjusts the system automatically.
    • Sensors do not make decisions‚Äîthey simply send data to a microprocessor which then determines the appropriate action.
  • Sensors are used in both monitoring systems (which observe and report) and control systems (which take action).
    • Monitoring systems only report when something is out of range, like a hospital monitor alerting a nurse.
    • Control systems adjust devices to bring the situation back to a desired level, like an air conditioner turning on when it's too hot.
    • Monitoring systems can alert users when abnormal values are detected, while control systems use sensor data to automatically adjust the output to maintain desired conditions.
  • Different types of sensors are used for specific applications:
  • Temperature sensors measure heat levels.
    • Used in greenhouses, heating systems, and industrial processes.
    • Can also be used in medical applications like digital thermometers or body temperature monitoring systems.
  • Moisture and humidity sensors detect water levels in air or soil.
    • Used in agriculture, food factories, and microchip manufacturing.
  • Light sensors measure brightness and trigger actions.
    • Used in automatic streetlights or headlamps on cars.
    • Light sensors are also used in alarm systems or display brightness control.
  • Infrared sensors detect invisible IR radiation.
    • Active sensors detect broken beams; passive sensors detect heat.
  • Pressure sensors measure force from solids, liquids, or gases.
    • Used in weighing systems and safety checks.
  • Acoustic sensors detect sound.
    • Used in security alarms or machinery fault detection.
  • Gas sensors detect oxygen or carbon dioxide levels.
    • Used in pollution monitoring or car emissions.
    • Gas sensors can also be used in safety systems to detect leaks in factories or households.
  • pH sensors measure how acidic or alkaline a substance is.
    • Used in soil testing and chemical plants.
  • Magnetic field sensors detect changes in magnetic strength.
    • Used in CD players, mobile phones, and braking systems.
  • Accelerometers detect motion or changes in velocity.
    • Used in airbag systems and phone screen rotation.
  • Proximity sensors detect nearby objects without touching them.
    • Used in mobile phones to turn off the screen when near the ear.
    • Proximity sensors are also used in parking systems to detect obstacles and alert drivers.
  • Flow sensors measure how much gas or liquid is moving.
    • Used in hospital devices and pipe flow systems.
  • Level sensors check liquid levels in tanks or containers.
    • Used in fuel tanks, medicine production, and leak detection.
  • In a smart greenhouse, a temperature sensor checks the air temperature and sends data to a microprocessor. If it's too hot, a fan is activated. The new temperature is then measured again, continuing the loop.
  • A thermostat uses a temperature sensor to maintain a set room temperature. If the room is too cold, it turns on the heating.
