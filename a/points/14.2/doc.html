Circuit Switching & Packet Switching

Theory notes prepared by Dr. Hamdeni

Introduction — What is “switching”?

Switching is the method a network uses to move data between devices. In circuit switching the network reserves a continuous path for the whole session. In packet switching the message is divided into packets that are forwarded hop by hop and reassembled at the destination. Each approach has distinct benefits, drawbacks, and typical uses.

14.2a Circuit switching

Dedicated end-to-end path

  • A circuit‑switched connection establishes one dedicated end‑to‑end route for the whole session, and every bit of traffic follows that single route.
  • Operation proceeds in three stages: circuit establishment, data transfer, connection termination.
  • The reserved circuit provides unimpeded bandwidth to the communicating parties while the session is active and cannot be used by other traffic.
  • Because a single route is used throughout, the data stream is delivered in order by design.

Benefits & drawbacks

  • BenefitGuaranteed bandwidth supports constant‑rate streams such as continuous audio.
  • DrawbackInefficiency when idle since capacity remains reserved even if no data is sent.
  • DrawbackA single fixed path can be a security risk if that line is compromised.

Examples & applicability

  • Traditional public telephone networks and dedicated leased lines.
  • ApplicabilityUsed when a continuous reserved circuit is required for the session.

14.2b Packet switching

Shared links, per‑packet forwarding

  • In packet switching a message is split into packets that travel independently across the network without reserving any end‑to‑end circuit.
  • Each packet header carries delivery information in addition to the data payload.
  • A router reads the destination IP address in the header, consults its routing table (a list that maps destinations to next‑hop choices), and forwards the packet one hop at a time toward its destination.
  • Shared links enable efficient resource use because many transmissions statistically share the same paths.
  • Resilience arises because packets can be forwarded along alternative routes if a link or node becomes unavailable.
  • At the receiver, packets are reassembled into the original message since they may arrive out of order.

Benefits & drawbacks

  • BenefitAccuracy and completeness improve through detection of missing packets and retransmission when required.
  • DrawbackExtra delay while lost packets are resent; greater protocol complexity; weaker real‑time timing guarantees without additional mechanisms.

Example & applicability

  • Fetching a web page where the data travels as separate packets that may take different routes and are reassembled by the browser.
  • ApplicabilityUsed for Internet data traffic where independent packets are forwarded hop by hop and reassembled at the destination.
